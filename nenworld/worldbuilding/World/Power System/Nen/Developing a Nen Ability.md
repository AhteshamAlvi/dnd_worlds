# Developing a Nen Ability

[[Ideal Power System For the Real World]] › [[Nen]] › [[Nen Ability]]

A [[Nen Ability]] is **grown, not granted.** Nobody — not a teacher, not the world, not the GM — hands a practitioner a finished ability with its stages already written. The user builds it themselves, out of who they are and what they have learned to do, and it earns new forms only as they earn them in the world. This page is the scaffold for that: a set of slots the player fills in, not a menu they pick from.

The rule underneath all of it comes from the [[Nen Ability]] and [[Conditions]] pages: an ability reflects its creator's character, and its limits are set by that person's own deepest sense of what's fair (their "internal locus of equivalence"). **You cannot fake this.** An ability that doesn't honestly fit the person who made it stays weak, no matter how clever it looks on paper.

## The scaffold

Fill these in **in order**. Later slots depend on earlier ones, the same way the [[The Nen Path|Path]] itself does.

1. **Seed** — Where the ability comes from. Two inputs: your [[Types of Nen|aura type]] (what your Nen is naturally good at) and your character (how you fight, what you value, what you're afraid of, what you can't let go of). The best seeds sit where those two overlap.

2. **Core effect** — One honest sentence: *what does the ability actually do?* Keep it true to your type and [[Types of Nen|affinities]] — an Emitter's core is not a Conjurer's. One clear effect beats five vague ones; everything else grows off it.

3. **Forms / Stages** — This is the part the player invents, and the part nobody writes for you. A "stage" (or form) is a named variation, escalation, or new mode of the core effect — think of a fighter who names each strike, or an ability that has a "first gear, second gear, third gear." **You do not design the whole ladder up front.** You start with one form, maybe two, and *the rest emerge in play.* Every new stage you propose has to pass two tests:
   - **Technique test** — it can only lean on techniques you have actually learned (see [[The Nen Path]]). A stage that needs [[En]] is impossible until you've learned En.
   - **Truth test** — it has to grow out of something you *actually do* — a way you've been fighting, a habit, a promise, a discovery about your own aura. It can't appear from nowhere just because it'd be strong.

4. **Conditions** — Parameters that make the ability feasible or sharpen it, paid by [[Conditions|equivalent exchange]]. These aren't punishments; they're the price and shape of the effect ("only works on someone whose name I know," "only while I'm touching the ground"). Honest, character-true conditions make an ability *stronger*, not weaker.

5. **Vows & Risk** — Optional, and where real power lives. Self-imposed restrictions ([[Vows and Limitations]]) and self-endangering stakes ([[Risk]]) amplify an ability well past its tier — the stricter the rule and the worse the penalty for breaking it, the bigger the boost. The catch is written on those pages: vows can be broken (and then the power's gone, or the penalty lands), and risk is real risk.

6. **Growth triggers** — How new stages actually unlock. This is the mechanism that keeps stage-writing out of the GM's hands: instead of the GM authoring your ladder, **the world validates the next rung.** A trigger is an in-fiction event that earns a new form:
   - keeping (or breaking) a creed you swore your ability on,
   - a hard-won victory, a rival overcome, a limit survived,
   - long deliberate practice on a specific idea,
   - an emotional turning point the ability was always about,
   - learning the *technique* a form you've been reaching for finally requires.

## How a stage "derives from what you do" — the loop

New forms come out of a repeating loop, and the player drives every step of it:

> **Do → Notice → Propose → Justify → It sticks.**
>
> You *do* something in play (fight a certain way, make a vow, discover a quirk of your aura). You *notice* a pattern worth crystallizing. You *propose* it as a new form or stage. You *justify* it against the two tests — you have the technique, and it grew from something real. If it passes, it *sticks* as a permanent new stage of your ability.

Because the trigger is always something that happened in the fiction, the GM never has to invent your stages — they only have to recognize when you've earned one.

## A blank worksheet

```
ABILITY NAME:            ____________________
Aura type & affinities:  ____________________
Seed (type × character): ____________________
Core effect (1 sentence):____________________

Forms / stages known so far:
  1) ______________  — leans on technique: ______  — grew from: ______
  2) ______________  — leans on technique: ______  — grew from: ______
  (leave room; more unlock in play)

Conditions:              ____________________
Vows / Risk (optional):  ____________________
Growth triggers I'm chasing: ________________
```

Start with the Seed, the Core effect, and **one** form. Everything else is meant to be blank on day one — that blank space is the point. Your ability is supposed to be unfinished, because it's supposed to grow.
