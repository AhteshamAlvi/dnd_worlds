Grief and rage alone were never enough to make an Infernal — that was always half the story. What actually turns someone is a disease: a smoldering infection that lies dormant in the body, waiting for a trigger it might never get. Someone who doesn't carry it can be as consumed by trauma as it is possible to be, and nothing happens beyond the ordinary damage grief does to a person. Igniting requires having the disease first.

The disease spreads two ways, and only two: through the bite or sting of a rare kind of insect that carries it, or through being burned by an Infernal already alight — their fire carries the same infection their own transformation once did, so surviving contact with one is its own kind of exposure.

It isn't a death sentence. The disease is curable, though rarely an easy or a cheap cure, and most people who catch it live with it quietly for years — sometimes a whole life — without ever combusting, so long as they never break in the specific, total way it demands. Igniting takes more than an infected person having a bad day: the grief, rage, or trauma has to be so overwhelming it consumes absolutely everything else about who they are, even if only for the moment it takes to combust. Most infected people never reach that point even once; the ones who do emerge as something built from fire instead of flesh.

Most Infernals lose themselves completely in the process: personality gone, memories gone, nothing left but flame and instinct given exactly enough humanoid shape to be recognized as having once been a person. It's this state that most people mean when they say "Infernal," and it's treated as a tragedy rather than a monster attack, even when it has to be put down as one.

A rare few Infernals resist full dissolution and hold on to a fragment of who they were — enough to still recognize loved ones, still speak, still choose. These lucid Infernals are exceptionally uncommon, live with the fire as a permanent part of their body rather than something they can set aside, and exist in a strange social space: not fully accepted as people by those who only know the mindless kind, but not willing to give up what's left of themselves either.

Because becoming an Infernal still requires a specific, personal breaking point on top of the disease, there's no Infernal homeland or population — only scattered individuals and the doctors, quarantines, and rare cures built around the disease that makes them possible.
