# Nen Manifestation (Spirit)

[[00 Read Me First|Rulebook]] › Aura Engine

This chapter defines the one thing **Spirit (SPI)** does. SPI is not a pool, a recovery rate, a knowledge stat, a control stat, or a ceiling — every one of those belongs to another attribute now ([[Attributes and Skills]]). SPI is **innate Nen talent**, and talent only shows itself at a single moment: when everything that *can* be prepared has been prepared, and it still isn't certain the Nen will take. That moment is a **Manifestation roll**.

## The principle

Most Nen is not a manifestation roll. Firing a known ability, holding [[Ken]], throwing a punch with [[Kō]] — those are settled by execution ([[Combat Core]]: DEX + proficiency) and resources ([[Aura Statistics]]). You roll SPI only when the outcome is **genuinely uncertain after preparation is complete** — creating something, binding something, stabilizing something, reaching for something unprecedented.

The order of operations makes it concrete. A manifestation has up to five gates, and **SPI is always the last**:

```
Knowledge     → WIS   Do you understand what you're attempting? (Nen Theory)
Manipulation  → DEX   Can your hands shape the aura precisely enough?
Resources     → AP/AO Do you have the aura the attempt physically costs?
Validity      → Rules Is the attempt legal under the technique/ability rules?
Manifestation → SPI   Given all of the above — does the Nen take?
```

Fail any of the first four and you don't reach the SPI roll at all: you don't understand it, can't shape it, can't afford it, or it's simply not a thing Nen can do. Clear all four and the SPI roll asks the only question left — **does your talent make it real this time?**

> **Manifestation roll = d20 + SPI mod + relevant proficiency, vs Difficulty.**

The proficiency is usually **Hatsu** (for abilities) or **Nen Theory** (for Contracts and rituals), GM's call by what the attempt leans on.

## When to call for it

| Attempt | Typical DC | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Awaken your first Hatsu** — the seed becomes a real ability ([[Hatsu Design]]) | 13 | The manifestation the whole first arc builds to |
| **Bind a new Condition or Vow** into an ability (making the equivalent-exchange *hold*) | 13–16 | Success seats it; failure means your own locus won't accept the terms yet ([[Conditions Vows and Risk (Rules)]]) |
| **Forge a Nen Contract** between people/things | 16 | The classic SPI set-piece; see below |
| **Stabilize an experimental effect** (a form you're deriving in play, [[Developing a Nen Ability]]) | 16–20 | Failure = the effect fizzles or destabilizes, not "you lose the idea" |
| **Manifest an unprecedented effect** (something no one at the table has seen work) | 20–24 | Reaching past your catalogued abilities |
| **Reality-adjacent Specialist work** (knowledge of the hidden, rule-effects at the edge of "human capacity", [[Specialization]]) | 24–28+ | GM-gated; often needs Conditions/Vows just to attempt |

Routine, repeated use of an already-manifested ability **never** rerolls this — once a Hatsu is real, it's real; you execute it with DEX. The SPI roll is for *becoming*, not *using*.

## Failure

Manifestation failure is dramatic, not punitive — the attempt was always a reach:

- **Fizzle** (miss the DC): the aura and the action are spent; nothing manifests. Try again when circumstances change.
- **Miss by 5+**: 1 [[Injury Recovery and Conditions|Strain]] — you forced something that wouldn't seat.
- **Natural 1 on a high-DC reach (20+)**: **backlash** — the unstable Nen recoils; GM picks a consequence proportional to the ambition (a Strain spike, a temporary Efficiency dip in that category, an effect that manifests *wrong* and has to be lived with until unmade).

Emotion and stakes help exactly as elsewhere ([[Conditions Vows and Risk (Rules)]]): a genuine emotional moment tied to the attempt grants **advantage**; real Risk in the attempt can grant a circumstance bonus. Talent (SPI) is the die; conviction bends it.

## Why SPI is rare-but-decisive

Because SPI is off the moment-to-moment sheet, two Nen users of identical pool, output, control, and technique can differ entirely in what they can *bring into being*. The high-SPI prodigy seats a new Condition on the first try, forges the Contract under pressure, and stabilizes the desperate new form mid-fight; the low-SPI grinder gets there too, but needs the calm, the prep, the second and third attempt. This is the fiction's "some people are just *gifted* at Nen" made mechanical without letting the gift touch raw power — a prodigy doesn't hit harder or last longer, they simply **make the impossible thing hold**.

Raising SPI is therefore the rarest advancement in the game and stays gated to extraordinary causes ([[Nen Growth]]): near-death, a sacred baptism, a decade-vow fulfilled, certain [[Devils Fruits (Rules)|fruits]] or relics. It buys no pool and no Soak — only better odds at the table's most important single roll.

## Worked example — forging a Contract

Two duelists want a **Nen Contract**: "whoever loses this duel leaves the city forever," self-enforcing on both.

1. **Knowledge (WIS):** the initiator must understand Contract-craft — Nen Theory DC 16. *Pass* — she studied it.
2. **Manipulation (DEX):** weaving two auras into one binding term — DEX + Hatsu DC 13. *Pass.*
3. **Resources:** the Contract costs a committed 200 aura from each party (it's a standing effect). *Both can afford it.*
4. **Validity:** a Contract needs the willing participation of both and a clear, equivalent term — met (both consent; the term is symmetric).
5. **Manifestation (SPI):** *now* she rolls **d20 + SPI + Hatsu vs DC 16.** Success: the Contract seats — it will enforce itself, and neither can cheat it (their own loci hold it, [[Conditions]]). Failure: the auras don't fuse; the words were just words, the aura is spent, and everyone felt it *not* take.

Nothing about steps 1–4 was a gamble — they were homework. Step 5 is where talent decided whether homework became a law of the world. That is the whole of what Spirit does.
